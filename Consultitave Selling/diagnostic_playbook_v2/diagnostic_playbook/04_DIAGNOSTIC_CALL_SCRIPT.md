# 04 — DIAGNOSTIC CALL SCRIPT

**Purpose:** Run a structured 30-minute conversation that produces (a) genuine value for the prospect, (b) clean transcript data for memo generation, (c) an emotional anchor that earns the right to follow up about BidIntell later.

**Tools:** Zoom + Granola (recording/transcription).

**Print this. Have it on your desk during the call.**

---

## BEFORE THE CALL — 5 MINUTE PREP

1. Open the prospect's intake form answers (Q4 — what they're hoping to get out of this)
2. Quick LinkedIn scan: company size, geographic footprint, any visible projects
3. Check for any past conversation history (if they're a warm contact)
4. Open Granola, confirm it's set to record
5. Open the diagnostic project in Claude in another tab (you may want to dictate notes during the call)
6. Have this script open in front of you

**You should know before the call:** their company name, role, trade, revenue range, and one-sentence concern. That's it. Don't over-prep — you want to react authentically to what they say.

---

## THE OPENING (2 MINUTES)

> "Thanks for the time, [Name]. Before we get into it, three quick things.
>
> One — I'm not selling anything on this call. I will not pitch BidIntell. The deliverable is a written memo I send you in a few days.
>
> Two — I'm recording this with Granola so I can write up your memo without trying to remember everything in real time. Recording stays private. Cool?
>
> Three — I'm going to ask 5 questions. Mostly I'll listen. The whole thing should take 30 minutes. If you want to go longer, we can — but 30 is the contract.
>
> Quick warm-up before the questions. Give me the 30-second version of your business. What do you do, where, and roughly what size are you?"

**Why this opening matters:**
- Reaffirms no-pitch promise (lowers their guard)
- Gets explicit consent for recording
- Sets the time boundary (they relax knowing it won't run long)
- The warm-up question is throwaway — purpose is to let them hear themselves talk and get comfortable

**Listen for in the warm-up:** Trade detail, geographic footprint, employee count if they share it, how they describe their business (problem-first or capability-first?). Don't interrupt. Let them go.

---

## QUESTION 1 — WINS (6 MINUTES)

> "Okay. Walk me through your last 5 wins. Doesn't have to be perfect recall — just the shape of them. For each one: which GC, what scope, why do you think you won it?"

**What you're listening for:**
- **Win concentration**: Are wins clumped with 1-2 GCs, or spread across many? (Concentrated = relationship moat, but also fragility if GC changes hands.)
- **Win attribution accuracy**: Do they actually know why they won, or are they guessing? "We won because we're good at what we do" = they don't know.
- **Win patterns**: Same scope type? Same GC type? Same project size? This is their unconscious "lane."

**Steering moves you might need:**
- If they only give 2 wins: "Anything else come to mind from the last 6 months? Even smaller jobs."
- If they're vague: "Take the most recent one — what was the project?"
- If they go too long on one: "That's helpful. What about the next one?"

**Your facilitator job:** Get specific names where possible (GCs, project types, dollar ranges). The memo lands harder when it references their actual GCs by name.

**DO NOT:**
- Comment on whether their reasoning is right
- Suggest what they should have noticed
- Bring up BidIntell features that would have helped

---

## QUESTION 2 — LOSSES (6 MINUTES)

> "Now your last 5 losses. Same format — GC, scope, what happened?"

**What you're listening for:**
- **Loss patterns**: Same competitor beating them repeatedly? Same GC where they keep losing? Same scope type they shouldn't be touching?
- **Self-awareness**: Do they know why they lost, or do they have explanations that sound like rationalizations? ("They went cheap" / "Bad numbers from our supplier" — these can be real, but if they're the answer to every loss, the prospect doesn't know what's happening.)
- **Variance vs signal**: 2 of 7 losses with one GC = variance. 7 of 7 losses with one GC = signal. Note when it's signal.

**Steering moves:**
- If they say "we just didn't get it" — push: "What do you think the actual reason was, even if you're guessing?"
- If they blame price every time — push: "Is it always price? Or is that the easy answer?"
- If they get defensive — pull back: "Yeah, fair. Losses are hard to remember clearly."

**The most valuable loss data is when they're losing to the same competitor or same GC repeatedly without realizing it.** That's the "you're a stalking horse" pattern — and it's something the memo can name explicitly.

---

## QUESTION 3 — THE REGRET BIDS (8 MINUTES — THIS IS THE GOLD)

> "Of the bids you chased over the last year — wins, losses, all of it — which ones do you wish you'd never started? Where you knew on day 3 you weren't going to win it but you'd already burned the estimating time?"

**This question changes the energy of the call.** Most owners have never been asked this directly. Watch for the shift in their voice when they answer it.

**What you're listening for:**
- **The story they tell first** — that's the deepest pain, told most viscerally
- **Specific GCs they regret** — name them in your notes
- **Hours/dollars wasted** — they may not volunteer numbers, but if they do, capture them
- **The pattern they almost see but haven't named** — your job in the memo is to name it for them
- **Emotional language** — "we got jerked around," "we should've known better," "I was pissed at myself" — write down the verbatim

**Steering moves:**
- If they only share one regret: "Yeah. What else? Anything else come to mind from the last 6 months?"
- If they go general ("we just chase too many bids in general"): "Take one specific example. The most recent one that stings."
- If they don't have any: PROBE HARDER — "Really? No bid in the last 12 months you wish you hadn't run?" Almost everyone has one. If they really don't, that's a finding too — they're either elite at bid selection or they have low self-awareness.

**THIS QUESTION IS WHERE THE MEMO COMES FROM.** Spend the time. If you go over by 2 minutes, you go over.

**DO NOT:**
- Solve the problem out loud
- Suggest BidIntell would have helped (you said you wouldn't pitch — and you won't)
- Validate their decision to chase the bid
- Judge their decision to chase the bid

You are a peer hearing the story, not a coach. Just listen and write.

---

## QUESTION 4 — THE DECISION PROCESS (5 MINUTES)

> "Walk me through last Tuesday morning. A bid invite hits your inbox. Talk me through exactly what happens — who looks at it, what you check, how you decide."

**What you're listening for:**
- **Who is the actual decision-maker?** Owner? Estimator? Committee? Default? (Often it's: "we just kind of decide together" — which means nobody decides.)
- **Time spent on bid/no-bid decision**: minutes? hours? often, "we just start the takeoff" — which means there is no bid/no-bid decision, just a default-yes
- **Tools or process**: Excel? Paper? Software? Just gut?
- **Triggers that would change a yes to a no**: GC reputation? Scope mismatch? Already busy? Geographic? They may not realize they have implicit triggers — your job is to surface them

**Steering moves:**
- If they say "we just look at it and decide": "Walk me through 'look at it.' What do you actually open first?"
- If they say "the estimator handles it": "And how does the estimator decide? What's their thought process?"
- If they describe a formal process: probe to confirm it's real, not aspirational. "Did that happen on the last 3 bids?"

**The honest answer here is almost always: there is no real decision process. The bid happens because the invite arrived and inertia did the rest.** That's the diagnostic finding for ~80% of subs. Don't act surprised — just capture it precisely.

---

## QUESTION 5 — THE WISH (3 MINUTES)

> "Last one. If you could see one thing about a bid invite the moment it landed — something you can't see today — what would it be?"

**This is your roadmap question.** Their answer goes into your product backlog whether they convert or not.

**What you're listening for:**
- **Specific data they wish they had**: GC behavior history? Win rate by scope? Competitor info? Contract risk?
- **Decision-support they're missing**: a recommendation? a score? a "this looks like the bids you've lost"?
- **Process they wish existed**: someone else doing the bid/no-bid for them? A way to log decisions?

**Steering moves:**
- If they give you a vague answer ("more information"): "What kind specifically?"
- If they give a list: "If you had to pick one — the highest-leverage one — what would it be?"
- If they say "I don't know" — they're tired or guarded. Try: "What's the most annoying part of getting a bid invite? Start there."

**Their verbatim answer to this question goes into the memo and the dataset.** It's also the line you may quote (anonymized) in a future LinkedIn post.

---

## THE CLOSE (3 MINUTES)

> "That's the whole thing. Five questions in 30 minutes — appreciate you running through it.
>
> A few quick logistics. I'll write up what I see across these 10 bids and send you a one-page memo in the next 3-5 business days. Anything specific you want me to dig into when I'm pulling the patterns?
>
> [Listen, take note]
>
> Cool. Last thing — a week or so after I send the memo, I'll follow up with one short email. That email will mention BidIntell because that's where this research feeds into. You can reply or ignore. Whatever's useful for you.
>
> Thanks for the time, [Name]. Talk soon."

**Why this close matters:**
- **You ask for any final asks** — this can surface a specific pain they didn't get to in the questions
- **You set up the follow-up email transparently** — no surprise pitch, no broken promise. They know it's coming.
- **You exit on a peer note**, not a sales note

**DO NOT:**
- Schedule a follow-up call here
- Mention pricing
- Mention features
- Offer to set up a trial
- Say "I think you'd really benefit from..."

The memo is the bridge. Trust it.

---

## IMMEDIATELY AFTER THE CALL

5 minutes:

1. Open the tracking sheet (file 07)
2. Fill in the call as fully as you can while it's fresh — quotes, energy rating, fit rating, notes for memo
3. Confirm Granola transcript saved
4. Drop transcript file into the diagnostic project in Claude
5. Block calendar time within 5 days to write the memo

**Do not skip the tracking sheet step.** This is where your dataset lives. If you wait until "later," half the texture will be gone.

## COMMON SITUATIONS

### They ask about BidIntell during the call

> "Happy to talk about it after the memo lands — that's the deal I made when I set this up. Right now I just want to listen."

Then redirect: "Back to the regrets question — you mentioned [GC name]. What else stands out?"

### They start pitching their own services to you

> "Appreciate that — let's circle back on that after the call. I'd actually love to hear more, but right now I want to make sure we get the diagnostic done so the memo is good."

Then redirect to the question.

### They're brief and giving 1-word answers

You have a guarded prospect. Three options:

1. **Slow down.** "Yeah. Take the most recent one specifically — walk me through it."
2. **Volunteer something first.** "Yeah, I had a bid like that. We went 0 for 6 with [generic GC type] before I figured out the pattern. Have you had anything like that?"
3. **Acknowledge.** "I'm asking direct questions and I know it can feel like an interview. Tell me to back off if I'm pushing too hard."

### They're a fountain and you can't get through 5 questions

Ride it for 10 minutes. Then redirect: "This is gold, [Name]. Let me steer us — I want to make sure we hit all 5 questions before we run out of time. On losses…"

### They turn philosophical or strategic

If they start talking about the industry, the future, business strategy, etc. — gently redirect to specific bids. "All true. Take a specific example from your own pipeline — last 3 months."

The memo only writes itself if you have specifics. General reflections are content; specifics are the memo.

### The call runs over 30 minutes

If they want to keep going, fine — but cap at 45. Beyond that, fatigue sets in for both of you and the data quality drops. End with: "I want to be respectful of your time. Let me write this up and send it your way."

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO ON THE CALL

1. **Do not open BidIntell in your browser.** Even if you intend not to share screen. The visual presence makes you accidentally reach for it.
2. **Do not take notes that the prospect can see.** If you screen-share, they edit themselves. Granola handles transcription; your job is presence.
3. **Do not nod along sympathetically to bad reasoning.** "That makes sense" is fine. "You're absolutely right to chase that bid" is not — you don't actually know yet.
4. **Do not promise the memo will fix anything.** Promise it will reflect back what you saw. That's a much smaller, more honest promise.
5. **Do not give them BidIntell-specific data on their bids.** "I could run this through BidIntell and show you" — NO. That's the follow-up email. The call is research.
