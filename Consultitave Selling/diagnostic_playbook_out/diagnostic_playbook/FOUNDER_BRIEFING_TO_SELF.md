# BRIEFING TO YOURSELF — READ BEFORE EVERY DIAGNOSTIC CALL

**Print this. Keep it on your desk. Read it 5 minutes before every diagnostic call.**

---

## THE THREE THINGS YOU WILL WANT TO DO BUT MUST NOT

**1. You will want to pitch BidIntell mid-call.**

The prospect will describe a problem and you will see, instantly, exactly how BidIntell solves it. The words will form in your mouth: "That's actually exactly what BidIntell does." Do not say them.

If you pitch in the call, you collapse the framework. The diagnostic stops being research and becomes the world's slowest sales pitch. Every prospect who has ever sat through a fake-research call knows the move. They'll lose trust in 90 seconds and you'll never get it back.

**The pitch is the follow-up email. The call is the call.**

**2. You will want to validate their decisions.**

When they describe a regret bid, your sympathy reflex will kick in. "Yeah, that GC is awful, you couldn't have known." Don't do this either.

You actually don't know whether they couldn't have known. Validating their decisions makes the memo dishonest later. The memo's value is that it tells them what an outside expert *actually* sees in their data — including patterns they should have caught.

You can be empathetic ("That's a tough spot") without validating the decision ("You did the right thing"). Be empathetic. Don't validate.

**3. You will want to fill silences.**

When you ask Question 3 — "Which bids do you wish you'd never started?" — there will be a pause. Sometimes 5 seconds. Sometimes 15. Your gut will scream that it's awkward and you'll want to add context or rephrase or move on.

**Don't.** That silence is the prospect deciding whether to tell you the real story or the polite one. If you fill it, they tell you the polite one. If you wait, they tell you the real one.

The hardest skill in this call is sitting in 10 seconds of silence with the question hanging.

---

## WHAT TO REMEMBER ABOUT YOURSELF

You spent 10 years on the GC side and 10 years building FSI's specialty sub division to $8M. You shut it down when it stopped being profitable — partly because you bid wrong too many times.

This is not a sales background. This is a *practitioner* background. The prospect across the table is your peer. They've made the same mistakes. They've been chased by the same GCs. They've stared at the same 7am bid invite and made the same default-yes decision.

When you walk into the call thinking "I'm a founder selling to a customer," you'll subtly perform. When you walk in thinking "I'm a sub owner talking to another sub owner about something we both lived," you'll be present.

**The prospect can tell the difference. So can you.**

---

## WHAT TO REMEMBER ABOUT THE PROSPECT

They didn't book this call to help you. They booked it because something in the offer hooked them — usually one of these:

- They're tired of bidding the same GC and losing every time
- An estimator quit and they're rethinking the workflow
- They're sizing up whether to hire another estimator or fix the process
- They saw your post and the framing felt accurate to their situation
- They like your background and wanted to talk to you, regardless of the offer

**Read their intake form Q4 answer (the "what are you hoping to get out of this") before the call.** That answer is the thing they're actually trying to solve. Steer the call's emphasis toward whichever question best surfaces it.

---

## THE FIVE QUESTIONS — WHY EACH ONE EXISTS

**Q1 (Wins):** Warm-up question. Easy to answer. Gets them comfortable. Tells you their unconscious lane and which GCs they consider safe.

**Q2 (Losses):** Pattern-detection question. Are they losing to the same competitor or same GC repeatedly? They probably haven't noticed the pattern themselves.

**Q3 (Regrets):** The gold. The emotional anchor. The thing the memo will be built around. Spend the most time here. Take the most notes. Sit in the silence.

**Q4 (Decision Process):** The diagnostic question. Almost always reveals there is no real decision process — bids happen by default. That's the finding for ~80% of subs.

**Q5 (The Wish):** Roadmap input for you, signal for the memo's closing line. What they wish they could see at bid invite moment is what BidIntell needs to surface. Their answer is gold for product, content, and follow-up.

If you only get through 4 of 5 questions, skip Q1, not Q3 or Q5.

## WHAT TO DO IF...

**...the prospect is brief and giving 1-word answers:**
Try this in order: (1) slow down and ask for one specific example, (2) volunteer something from your own time at FSI as a peer offering, (3) explicitly acknowledge the format is intense and let them know they can tell you to back off.

**...the prospect won't stop talking and you can't get through 5 questions:**
Ride one or two answers for color, then redirect with: "This is gold, [name]. Let me steer us so we hit all 5 before we run out of time. On losses..."

**...the prospect starts asking about BidIntell:**
"Happy to talk about it after the memo lands — that's the deal I made when I set this up. Right now I just want to listen."

**...the prospect gets emotional / opens up about real business stress:**
Be a peer. "Yeah, that's a hard spot. I've been there — different specifics, same texture. Let's keep going if you're up for it." Don't pivot to product.

**...you blank on the next question:**
The script is on your desk. Look at it. There's no shame in pausing to check the script — it actually signals to the prospect that you're running a structured thing, not freelancing.

**...you go over 30 minutes:**
Cap at 45. Past 45, fatigue degrades the data. End with: "I want to be respectful of your time. Let me write this up and send it your way."

**...the prospect asks "what does the memo cover":**
"The shape of how you pick bids today, three patterns I noticed across the 10 we walked through, the bid you regretted reflected back, and two suggestions you can act on this month. Plus one closing line on what I'd want to see in your decision process."

**...the prospect asks "can I share the memo with my partner":**
Yes, absolutely. Encourage it. The memo getting passed around is exactly what you want.

## WHAT TO DO IMMEDIATELY AFTER THE CALL — DON'T SKIP

5 minutes after the call ends, before you do anything else:

1. Open the tracking sheet. Fill in as much as you remember while it's fresh — quotes, fit rating, energy rating, notes for the memo.
2. Confirm Granola transcript saved. Drop it into the diagnostic project in Claude.
3. Block calendar time within 5 days to write the memo.
4. Put the prospect's name on a sticky note where you'll see it Monday morning, with the date the memo is due.

If you skip the tracking sheet step "to do later," half the texture will be gone by tonight. The 5 minutes after the call are the most valuable 5 minutes of the entire diagnostic motion.

## A REMINDER ABOUT THE BIGGER PICTURE

You are not running these diagnostics to convert prospects.

You are running these diagnostics to build the largest structured ICP research dataset on specialty subcontractor bid decision-making in commercial construction. The conversion is a byproduct.

In 6 months, when you have 30 of these diagnostics in the books, you'll have something no competitor has — and something Procore would want to acquire. Every diagnostic is a deposit into that asset.

Some will convert. Some won't. The diagnostic still has value either way.

That's what lets you walk into the call without being attached to the outcome. **Without that detachment, you'll pitch. With it, you'll be the most credible voice in the conversation.**

---

## ONE LAST THING

If the prospect doesn't convert, the diagnostic still worked. Their feedback shaped the product. Their voice is in the dataset. The memo is in their inbox and might get shared with three other sub owners over the next year.

You are playing a long game. Each call is one move.

**Now go run the call.**
