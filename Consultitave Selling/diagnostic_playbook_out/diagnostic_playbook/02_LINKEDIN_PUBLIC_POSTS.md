# 02 — LINKEDIN PUBLIC POSTS (LEAD MAGNET CHANNEL)

**Purpose:** Drive inbound diagnostic bookings from your LinkedIn network.
**Frequency:** 1 post per week dedicated to the diagnostic offer (rotate with your existing 2-3x/week content cadence).
**Voice:** Confidently Boring. Personal profile, not company page (company page gets 1.6% organic reach — your personal profile is the channel).

---

## RULES FOR THESE POSTS

1. Lead with the problem, not the offer
2. Make the offer specific (number of slots, who it's for, what they get)
3. Include a clear CTA — "comment 'diagnostic'" or "DM me"
4. No exclamation points
5. No emojis
6. Keep posts under 300 words. LinkedIn cuts at "see more" around line 3 — front-load the hook.

---

>

>
> What you get: walk through your last 10 bids with me, then a written one-page read on your selection patterns. Specific GCs and scopes I'd flag based on your data.
>
> What I get: pattern data across the trade. I'm building software in this space and the only way to build it right is talking to the people doing the work.
>
> No pitch attached. I'll confirm that in writing.
>
> Comment "diagnostic" or DM me if you want one of the slots.

## POST 2 — THE OBSERVATION (USE 1 WEEK LATER)

> Started the diagnostic conversations last week. Already a pattern showing up that surprised me.
>
> Almost every sub I've talked to so far can name the GC they should stop bidding. They know which one. They know the win rate is 1 in 8. They know the contracts are punishing.
>
> They're still bidding them.
>
> Not because they want to. Because the invite hits, the estimator opens it, and three hours later the takeoff is half-done and the inertia carries it the rest of the way. There's no decision moment built into the workflow. The bid happens because nobody stopped it.
>
> A few diagnostic slots still open this month. 30 minutes, walk through your last 10 bids, get a written read on your selection patterns. Free. No pitch on the call.
>
> DM me or comment "diagnostic."

**Why this post works:** It demonstrates that the diagnostic produces real insight (you've already done some), and the observation itself is the hook — it makes readers think about which GC *they're* stuck bidding.

## POST 3 — THE FRAME-FLIP (USE 2-3 WEEKS IN)

> Estimators are great at telling you why they won.
>
> They're worse at telling you why they lost.
>
> They're worst of all at telling you why they bid in the first place.
>
> "We had capacity." "We've worked with that GC before." "The drawings looked clean." Those aren't decisions. They're justifications written after the fact.
>
> The actual decision — should we spend 8 hours of estimating time on this invite — almost never gets made deliberately. It just happens.
>
> Running 30-minute bid selection diagnostics with specialty sub owners ($2M–$20M). Walk through your last 10 bids. I send you a written read on your selection patterns. Free.
>
> A few slots left. DM me.

## POST 4 — THE CASE STUDY (USE AFTER 5+ DIAGNOSTICS DONE)

> Quick case from the diagnostic conversations.
>
> Owner of a flooring sub, ~$6M revenue. Walked through his last 10 bids with him. Wins were spread across 4 GCs he'd worked with for years. Losses were almost all from one GC he'd never won with — 0 for 7 in 18 months.
>
> Asked him why he kept bidding them. He said: "They send a lot of invites and the work would be good if we got it."
>
> But he won't get it. The 0 for 7 isn't variance. It's a signal. Probably a stalking horse situation, probably a competitor with a relationship he can't break into.
>
> Cost him about 60 hours of estimating time over those 7 bids. At his estimator's rate, that's real money for a $6M shop.
>
> If you're an owner of a $2M–$20M specialty sub and you want a written read on what your last 10 bids actually say about how you're picking work, comment "diagnostic" or DM me. Free 30-minute call. No pitch.
>
> A few slots open this month.

**Anonymize ruthlessly.** No names, no GCs identifiable. The trade can be specific if it doesn't out the source.

## CTA HANDLING — WHEN PEOPLE COMMENT OR DM

When someone comments "diagnostic" on a post:

**Public reply (same day):**
> Sent you a DM.

**DM (immediately after):**
> Hey [Name] — Ryan. Thanks for the comment. Here's my Calendly: [link]. Pick a time that works. There's a short intake form on the booking page — takes 60 seconds. Looking forward to it.

**Why this works:** The public "sent you a DM" reply is *social proof* for everyone else watching the post. It signals: "this offer is real, people are taking it, and the founder is responsive."

---

## CADENCE INTEGRATION WITH EXISTING CONTENT PLAN

Your existing Q2 2026 content plan calls for 2-3 LinkedIn posts per week from your personal profile. Slot the diagnostic posts in as **one of the 2-3 weekly posts**, not on top.

**Recommended pattern:**

- Monday: Problem-focused content (existing plan)
- Wednesday: Diagnostic post (rotate from this file)
- Friday: Case/story/observation (existing plan)

Don't post about the diagnostic more than once a week. It will start feeling like a sales pitch and you'll lose the credibility that makes it work.

In the tracking sheet (file 07), log:

After 4-6 posts, you'll know which framing pulls best. Double down on that pattern. Drop the ones that don't.
