# SAMPLE DIAGNOSTIC MEMO — REFERENCE

**This is a fictional 1-page diagnostic memo for a fictional prospect (Mike Carlson, Cornerstone Flooring). Use it as the reference of what "good" looks like before you write your first real one.**

**Why a fictional sample matters:** The first memo you write will be twice as long as it needs to be, will read as too generic, and will accidentally pitch BidIntell. This sample shows the texture and constraint of a memo that actually works.

**Read this once. Then write your first real memo. Then come back and compare.**

# Cornerstone Flooring — Bid Selection Read

*Prepared by Ryan Elder, BidIntell — May 14, 2026*

---

**The shape of your bid selection.** You're running an effective trust-based selection process for the GCs you've worked with for years (Carrothers, Mid-States, J.E. Dunn local) — your win rate inside that group is solid and your team knows the work. Outside that group, you're bidding on default. The invite hits, Steve opens it, and the question of whether to commit estimating hours is answered by whether his calendar has room — not by whether the bid is winnable.

**Three patterns I noticed.**

First, the Henderson Construction situation. You said it yourself: "We've been bidding them for two years and we've gotten one job — and the one we got, we lost money on." That's not a relationship that's about to turn around. Henderson is sending you invites because their bid list requires three subs, and you keep filling slot three. The work would be good if you got it, but the math says you won't, and the one time you did, the contract terms ate the margin. Stop bidding Henderson unless they call you specifically and reference a project they want you on.

Second, your wins concentrate in healthcare and education — about 7 of your last 10 wins. Your losses are almost evenly split across all building types. There's a real signal here: when a healthcare or education invite hits, your odds are dramatically better than when an industrial or retail invite hits. You don't have to bid every trade your shop technically does. The shop is set up to win healthcare flooring, not to win every flooring job in Kansas City.

Third, your "we had capacity" rationale is doing a lot of work. Three of the five regret bids you walked through started with that line. Capacity is not a reason to bid — capacity is a reason it's *possible* to bid. The reason has to come from the bid itself. When you find yourself thinking "we have capacity, let's chase it," that's the moment to ask the real question: would I bid this if my calendar were full?

**The bid you regretted.** The Crossroads office tower. You said you knew on day 4 you weren't going to win it — Henderson was the GC, the spec called for a flooring system you've installed twice in 12 years, and the schedule was already compressed before bid day. But Steve had the time, the takeoff was started, and you finished it because nobody pulled the plug. Lost the bid by 8% to a sub you've never heard of, and burned roughly 14 hours of Steve's time. That bid had three signals against it before estimating started: wrong GC, wrong scope for your shop, wrong schedule. The decision to walk should have been made at hour two, not after the bid was submitted.

**Two suggestions.**

One, build a "signals" checklist Steve runs before he opens a takeoff. Not a complicated one. Three lines: Have we won with this GC in the last 24 months? Is this our scope, or are we forcing it? Does the schedule give us a chance? If the answer is no on any two of three, the bid does not start. You can do this with index cards on Steve's desk this week. No software required.

Two, track your ghost rate by GC for 90 days. You mentioned a few GCs that "send invites and never close out the awards" — your gut on which ones is probably right but the order will surprise you. Once you have the list ranked, the bottom three GCs come off your invite list permanently.

**What I'd want to see.** When a bid invite hits Steve's inbox at 9am Tuesday, the moment-of-truth data isn't "what's the scope" — it's "what's our historical win rate with this GC, in this scope, at this size." Until you have that surface in front of you when the decision is being made, you're going to keep bidding on default in the gray-area cases.

— Ryan

## WHAT TO NOTICE ABOUT THIS MEMO

Read it once for content, then read it a second time noticing the structure:

**Length:** 540 words. Readable in under 4 minutes. The 1-page memo is meant to be read at one sitting, not skimmed.

**Specificity:** Names a real (fictional) GC by name (Henderson), a real (fictional) employee by name (Steve), a real (fictional) regret bid (Crossroads office tower), and real (fictional) numbers (14 hours, 8% loss margin, 7 of 10 wins). When the memo hits the prospect's inbox, it has to feel like it was written about *them*, not about subs in general. Generic memos don't convert.

**Voice:** No exclamation points. No "I think you should." No "great opportunity to improve." Direct, peer-to-peer, occasionally blunt ("Stop bidding Henderson"). Estimator-talking-to-estimator.

**The verbatim quote:** "We've been bidding them for two years and we've gotten one job — and the one we got, we lost money on." Putting the prospect's own words back in front of them is one of the most powerful moves in this format. They told you something true. Reflecting it back makes the memo feel honest, not invented.

**The regret section:** Has the most narrative texture of any section. That's deliberate. The regret bid is the emotional anchor — it's the moment in the call where the prospect dropped their guard and said something real. The memo earns its keep by reflecting that moment back precisely.

**The two suggestions:** Practical, free, and explicitly NOT product-shaped. "Index cards on Steve's desk." "Track ghost rate for 90 days." If a prospect followed these suggestions to the letter and never bought BidIntell, they'd still be better off. That's the whole point. The memo has to provide real value standalone — that's what earns the right to the follow-up email.

**What's NOT in the memo:**
- No mention of BidIntell anywhere
- No software recommendations
- No "schedule a follow-up call" CTA
- No pricing
- No "I'd love to talk about how we can help"
- No emojis or exclamation points
- No section labeled "Recommendations" (sounds like a consultant's deliverable; this isn't that)
- No bullet lists outside the suggestions section

**The closing line:** "What I'd want to see" is the bridge to the follow-up email. The memo ends by naming the data gap, which is exactly the gap the follow-up email's "send me one bid invite, I'll score it" offer fills. The setup is done in the memo so the follow-up doesn't have to do it from scratch.

## TIME TO PRODUCE THIS MEMO

For reference: a memo of this quality takes roughly 25-35 minutes to write from a Granola transcript using the Claude prompt in file 05.

- 5 min reading the transcript
- 10 min Claude generating draft
- 10 min editing for voice, specificity, and the quote
- 5 min final pass for length and any pitch creep that snuck in

The first memo you write will probably take 60 minutes. By memo 3 you'll be at 30. By memo 5 it's a habit.

## WHAT BAD LOOKS LIKE — AVOID

For contrast, here's what a *bad* version of the same memo would look like (so you can spot it in your own first drafts):

> **Cornerstone Flooring — Bid Strategy Recommendations**
>
> Thank you for the opportunity to discuss your bid selection process. Based on our conversation, I have identified several areas where Cornerstone could improve its bid efficiency and win rate.
>
> **Recommendations:**
>
> 1. **Improve bid qualification.** Many subs in your position bid too many opportunities without proper qualification. Consider implementing a structured qualification process to ensure you're only pursuing winnable bids.
>
> 2. **Focus on relationships.** Your data suggests stronger performance with established GC relationships. Consider doubling down on these relationships and reducing exposure to less-known GCs.
>
> 3. **Leverage technology.** Tools like BidIntell can help you score opportunities before committing estimating resources, helping you make data-driven bid/no-bid decisions.
>
> Looking forward to continuing the conversation. Please let me know if you'd like to schedule a follow-up to discuss how BidIntell can support your bid selection process.

**Why it's bad:**
- Generic — could be sent to any sub in any trade
- "Several areas" / "many subs in your position" — anonymizing language that signals the writer doesn't actually know this prospect
- Pitches BidIntell directly (rule #1 broken)
- Has a follow-up CTA (rule #2 broken)
- Uses "leverage" and "data-driven" — banned voice words
- Numbered list with bold lead-ins — consultant-speak, not peer-to-peer
- No verbatim quote, no specific GC, no specific regret, no specific employee

If a draft of yours starts looking like this, throw it out and start over. Don't try to edit it. The whole frame is wrong.
