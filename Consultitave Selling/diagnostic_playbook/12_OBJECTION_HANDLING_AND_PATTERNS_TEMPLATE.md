# 12 — OBJECTION HANDLING

**Purpose:** Pre-built responses to the most common pushback at every stage of the diagnostic motion.
**Use:** Reference this in real-time when prospects push back. Update it as you encounter new objections.

---

## OBJECTIONS AT THE OUTREACH STAGE

### "I'm too busy"

> Totally get it. The standing offer is open whenever — if a quieter week shows up in the next month or two, just reply and we'll grab time. No follow-up pressure from me in the meantime.

**Then:** Mark "deferred" in tracking sheet. Try again in 60 days.

### "What's this for?"

> Short version: I built BidIntell because I lived this problem on the sub side for years and didn't have the tools I needed. Before I keep building, I'm sitting down with 30 specialty sub owners to make sure the way I think about bid selection actually matches how you do.
>
> The call is 30 minutes. I ask 5 structured questions about your last 10 bids — wins, losses, regrets, your decision process. After the call I write you a 1-page memo on what I see in your selection patterns. You keep that whether anything else happens or not.
>
> No pitch on the call. Worth a half hour?

### "Send me more info first"

> Happy to. Honest answer though — there isn't really more info to send beyond what I described. The diagnostic is the conversation. The deliverable is the memo. If 30 minutes feels like too much without seeing more, that's reasonable, but I don't have a brochure for this. The whole thing IS the brochure.
>
> Want to grab a 15-minute intro call instead? I can walk you through what the 30-min looks like and you can decide from there.

### "Is this a sales call?"

> Fair question. The 30 minutes is research, not sales. I will not pitch BidIntell on the call. Period.
>
> What I will do, separately, about a week after I send you the memo: send you a short follow-up email that mentions BidIntell because that's where this research feeds. You can reply or ignore. The memo and the call are yours either way.
>
> Still worth half an hour?

### "Why should I trust you with my bid data?"

> Reasonable question. Three things:
>
> 1. We're not on a screen-share. You're talking through bids verbally — no document upload, no data leaving your office.
>
> 2. The memo is anonymized for any cross-prospect pattern work I do. Your specifics never travel.
>
> 3. I built and ran a sub for ten years. Your bid data isn't useful to me as a competitor — I'm not bidding the same work. It's only useful to me as research input.
>
> If you want it in writing, I'll send a one-paragraph confidentiality note before the call.

### "Can you just send me a sample memo?"

> I can't share another shop's memo — confidentiality matters and they trusted me with their bid data. But I'd rather just run yours. Worth half an hour to find out?

### "I tried BidIntell already and it didn't work for me"

> Good — that's exactly the kind of context I need. The call would be even more useful with that history. I want to hear what didn't work. The memo will reflect what I see in your bid selection regardless of whether you ever come back to BidIntell.

### Silence (no reply at all)

After 7 days, send ONE nudge:

> Hey [Name] — pinging the diagnostic email back to the top of your inbox. No worries if it's not the right time. Door's open.
>
> Ryan

After that, stop. Do not send a third email. Mark "deferred" and try again in 60-90 days with a different hook.

## OBJECTIONS DURING THE CALL

### They start asking about BidIntell features

> Happy to talk about it after the memo lands — that's the deal I made when I set this up. Right now I just want to listen. Back to the regrets question — you mentioned [GC name]. What else stands out?

### They give vague answers and won't get specific

Three escalating moves:

**Move 1 — slow down:**
> "Yeah. Take the most recent one specifically — walk me through it."

**Move 2 — volunteer first:**
> "Yeah, I had a bid like that. We went 0 for 6 with [generic GC type] before I figured out the pattern. Have you had anything like that?"

**Move 3 — acknowledge:**
> "I'm asking direct questions and I know it can feel like an interview. Tell me to back off if I'm pushing too hard."

### They want to talk about industry trends instead of their own bids

> "All true. Take a specific example from your own pipeline — last 3 months."

### They start critiquing your script ("these are weird questions")

> "Yeah, they're not the typical questions. The five-question structure is intentional — it's how I get to patterns that don't show up in normal sales conversations. Stick with me on it. We're 12 minutes in, three questions left."

### They go over time

If they want to keep going past 30 min, fine — cap at 45. After 45:

> "I want to be respectful of your time. Let me write this up and send it your way. We can pick up the rest in the memo response."

### They turn it into a pitch for THEIR business / want you to be a customer

> "Appreciate that. Let's table it until after the diagnostic — I want to keep the 30 min focused on your bid selection. After we wrap, happy to hear what you've got."

### They get emotional / open up about real business stress

This happens more than you'd expect. An owner walking through regret bids sometimes opens up about cash flow, family pressure, business pain.

**Don't pivot to product.** Be a peer. Acknowledge:

> "Yeah. That's a hard spot to be in. I've been there — different specifics, same texture. Let's keep going if you're up for it. If you want to wrap early and pick up later, totally fine too."

If they want to keep going, finish the diagnostic. The memo for someone who opened up will be the most valuable one you write.

## OBJECTIONS AFTER THE MEMO

### "Some of this isn't quite right"

GREAT response. Reply:

> Appreciate the correction — that's exactly the kind of feedback I want. I'll update my notes. The follow-up next week will reflect the corrected read.
>
> Ryan

Then update tracking sheet. High-engagement = high conversion potential.

### "This doesn't tell me anything I didn't know"

This is real and worth acknowledging:

> Fair. The memo is meant to surface what you already know in writing — sometimes that's enough to act on, sometimes it isn't. The next step (if useful) is making the data part of how you decide on each bid, not just an annual reflection. That's what BidIntell does. Want me to score one of your real invites so you can see the difference?

This response converts the "no new info" objection into the BidIntell pitch — naturally.

### "I don't have time to read 5 pages"

> Totally fair. The page-4 GC-by-GC table is the highest-leverage piece. Read that one and skip the rest if you want.

### "Can you do this for my partner / estimator?"

> Sure. Send me their email and I'll loop them in. The diagnostic is built around the decision-maker though — if the bid/no-bid call ultimately stops with you, you should be in the call too.

## OBJECTIONS AT THE FOLLOW-UP / CONVERSION STAGE

### "I'm not ready to commit to software"

> Totally get it. The "send me a bid invite" offer doesn't require commitment — no signup, no card, no trial. I score it for you, send the analysis, and you decide if there's anything there worth pursuing.

### "How do I know this isn't going to take me forever to learn?"

> Fair concern. Most users get value out of their first 3 bid analyses. The first one I'll walk through with you on a 15-minute call so you don't have to figure it out alone. After that, it's a 5-minute upload and 30 seconds to read the score.

### "I'm using [competitor / spreadsheet / nothing] right now"

If spreadsheet/nothing:
> Yeah, that's where most subs are. The question is whether what you have today is producing the bid selection patterns you want. Based on the memo, my read was [reference specific pattern]. Worth running one bid through BidIntell side-by-side with your current process to see the difference?

If a competitor (rare — there aren't many direct ones):
> Tell me what you're using. Genuinely curious. If it's working, great — if there are gaps, I can tell you whether BidIntell fills them or not.

### "Can I see the actual product first?"

> Absolutely. Send me a bid invite and I'll do better than show you a demo — I'll send you back the actual scored output on your real work. That's the demo.

### "$99/month feels like a lot for one tool"

> Fair. The math: at $99/month you're getting [X] bid analyses. If even one of them keeps you from chasing a regret bid like the one we talked about — say 8-12 estimating hours saved — the tool pays for itself for the year in a single decision.
>
> The founding member rate is 30% off for life — code FOUNDER30. So you're at $69/month if you start while the founding offer is open. That's three takeout dinners.

### "I'll think about it"

> Sounds good. I'll leave the door open. If you want to send a bid invite in the next month or so, the offer stands.
>
> Ryan

Then stop. Mark "warm — re-engage 60 days." Don't push.

### "I don't think it's a fit"

> Appreciate you saying so directly. Mind sharing what felt off? Even one sentence helps me understand what's not working — it's the kind of feedback I can't get any other way.

If they share: capture in tracking sheet under "Reason didn't convert." This is gold for refining the offer.

If they don't reply: leave it. Mark closed. Move on.
